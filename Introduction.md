**Introduction**

*Salmonella* Typhimurium is associated with foodborne infections and is one of the main non-typhoidal serovars of *Salmonella enterica* isolated (Perez-Sepulveda & Hinton 2025). In particular, 
*S*. Typhimurium causes gastroenteritis in humans, and it has a broad host range and replicates in the intestine (Galán 2021). 
Animal reservoirs and products are likely to harbor instances of S. Typhimurium. S. Typhimurium variants have been isolated from poultry, and 
monophasic *S*. Typhimurium is strongly associated with the swine food chain (Liao, Lei, Liu, Tang & Peng 2026; Sun, Wan, Du & Bai 2020). Variants 
of *S*. Typhimurium have also been found to use cattle and wild birds as hosts, and particularly invasive infection of non-typhoidal *Salmonella* such as *S*. Typhimurium 
has an estimated disease burden of up to 129.5 million cases each year, leading to 100,000 to 1 million deaths per year (Branchu, Bawn & Kingsley 2018). 

As *S*. Typhimurium causes infections worldwide, genetic variation between populations in different hosts and regions is likely to occur. 
Broad-host and host-adapted S. Typhimurium variants have differing geographical distributions, with broad-host epidemic clones DT9, DT204, 
and DT104 dominant in cattle, pigs, and poultry across Europe over the past decades, while host-adapted variants are more limited in distribution, 
such as ST313 strains associated with invasive infections in sub-Saharan Africa (Branchu et al. 2018). Whole genome sequencing allows the 
identification of genetic variation between these variants. This project aims to compare high-impact gene variants in *S*. Typhimurium genomes 
sampled in various geographical regions and elucidate adaptations faced by each genome.
