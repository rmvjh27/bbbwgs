## Discussion
The genome of *S*. Typhimurium has been known to consist of a chromosome around 4.8 Mb long and a virulence plasmid around 90 kB long, an example of 
which being strain LT2 with a 4.857-Mb chromosome and a 93.939-kb plasmid (McClelland et al. 2001). The virulence plasmid is evident across different strains 
of S. Typhimurium. For instance, strain LT2 has a plasmid known as pSLT which is an invariable part of the strain’s genome, as it is carried by all lines 
of the strain and persists despite the strain being in culture for many years and subjected to numerous single-colony isolations (Sanderson, Hessel & Rudd 1995). 
Another example of the *S*. Typhimurium virulence plasmid is pSTUK-100 in strain UK-1 (universal killer-1), which exhibits a similar number of open reading frames 
and pseudogenes to pSLT (Luo et al. 2011). The virulence plasmid in *S*. Typhimurium and the *Salmonella* genus as a whole is characterized by a signature locus 
that consists of the *spvABCD* operon and its positive regulator gene *spvR*, with gene products that inhibit inflammatory and innate immune responses of the host 
during infection (Silva, Puente & Calva 2017).

SNPs being the predominant type of variant in all genomes goes in accordance with the conclusions made by (Pang et al. 2013), who found that variation between 
*S*. Typhimurium genomes is largely caused by accumulation of SNPs. Acquisition of SNPs can occur during chronic *S*. Typhimurium infection, which allows rapid genetic 
adaptation for long-term asymptomatic host-association (Søndberg & Jelsbak 2016). When infecting a human host, *S*. Typhimurium can acquire, over time, SNPs in genes with 
ontologies pertaining to metabolism, storage and processing of genetic material, as well as cellular processes and signalling (Sophie, Qinning, M, Vitali & Ruiting 2015). 
Upstream variants, prevalent in all genomes studied in this project, indicate SNPs in promoter and 5’ untranslated regions. Promoters are regions located upstream of genes, 
where bacterial RNA polymerase and sigma factors associate to initiate transcription, while 5’ untranslated regions occur between transcription and translation start sites 
and can harbour additional regulatory elements (Kröger et al. 2012). Variants in promoters upregulate or downregulate transcription levels without changing the structure 
of the genes transcribed.

Downstream variants are variants located after the 3’ end of a gene (Butkiewicz & Bush 2016). These variants may disrupt 3’ untranslated regions, which play a role in 
the post-transcriptional regulation of gene expression as they can act as targets of small RNAs (sRNAs), which cause degradation or stabilization of mRNA (Ren, Guo & Sun 2017). 
Similar to upstream variants, these variants affect gene expression levels without changing the gene’s sequence. The most prevalent variants directly changing gene sequences 
in all genomes studied are synonymous variants, which do not cause changes in the protein encoded by the affected genes, and missense variants that cause amino acid changes 
(Butkiewicz & Bush 2016).

UniProtKB mapping indicates that the *oadA* gene, for which all genomes studied have variants of, encodes for the alpha chain of oxaloacetate decarboxylase (OAD), 
with its gene ontology being sodium ion transport. OAD is a sodium pump that maintains the sodium gradient of microorganisms by using free energy from the decarboxylation of 
oxaloacetate to drive sodium transport across the membrane, its alpha chain catalyzing carboxyl transfer from oxaloacetate to biotin (Xu et al. 2020). This indicates that 
all genomes studied may have differences in sodium transport and citrate fermentation compared to the reference genome. All genomes also have variants in the *ydiV* gene, 
which encodes the EAL-like protein YdiV with cyclic-guanylate-specific phosphodiesterase activity and has a gene ontology in regulation of single-species biofilm formation. 
It is likely that all genomes have differences in pathogenicity to the reference genome due to variants of these two genes.

Four of the six genomes studied likely exhibit differences to the reference genome in defenses against bacteriophages due to variants in the *ygcB* gene. This gene encodes 
a helicase with RNA helicase activity and its gene ontology corresponds to defense response to viruses. Variants in this gene likely arise from defensive adaptation to bacteriophages 
that vary in identity from one region to another. The *artJ* gene encodes a component of the 3rd arginine transport system in the bacterial periplasm. This system plays a role in arginine uptake; 
the ArtJ component specifically binds arginine with high affinity (Wissenbach et al. 1995). Variants of this gene in genomes SRR39099216 and ERR023773 possibly indicate increased or 
decreased uptake or arginine in environments where samples of these genomes were taken. Both genomes also have variants for the *macB* gene, which may indicate increased antibiotic resistance in in 
these genomes as an adaptation to heavy antibiotic use in animal husbandry or clinical settings. This gene encodes the macrolide export ATP-binding/permease protein MacB, which is an 
ATP-binding cassette (ABC) family transporter that transports antibiotics out of the cell by forming a tripartite efflux pump with proteins MacA and TolC (Okada et al. 2017).

The *nanH* gene, variants of which found in genomes SRR38876376 and SRR38943380, encodes sialidase. This enzyme catalyzes the the removal of sialic acid from glycans on the surfaces
of host epithelial cells, allowing invasion of the host’s colon epithelium (Arabyan et al. 2016). Therefore, it is likely that these genomes have altered capabilities for glycan degradation, 
allowing more colon epithelial invasion. Meanwhile, genomes SRR38895580 and ERR023773 likely faced the same environmental pressures in their natural selection, indicated by variants for *lpfD*, *treC*, 
and *ybfE*. These genes have ontologies pertaining to cell adhesion involved in single-species biofilm formation, trehalose catabolism, and regulation of transcription, respectively. Both genomes 
also have variants of *ygbE*, but the function of this gene is yet to be elucidated.

The genome SRR39099216 is characterized by variants in *bcsG*, *dmsD*, *fepE*, and *mdtK*. The *bcsG* gene ontology indicates a role in cellulose biosynthesis, and thus biofilm formation. 
Variants of this gene may indicate a change in whether biofilm formation is needed in the environment of this genome’s population, as deletions in this gene, which encodes a transferase that adds 
phosphoethanolamine to cellulose during its synthesis, leads to weaker biofilms being formed (Anderson, Burnett, Hiscock, Maly & Weadge 2020). The *dmsD* gene encodes a chaperone involved in the 
maturation of specific enzymes, such as DMSO reductase, and belongs to a class of proteins with the same function with other oxidoreductases (Li, Chang, Howell & Turner 2010). High-impact variants 
may indicate adaptation in respiration that changes whether redox enzymes involving DmsD in their folding is required for survival. *fepE* allows the production of lipopolysaccharides with long O-antigens, 
contributing to bile acid resistance of *S*. Typhimurium in the inflamed intestine (Iduu, Kitchens, Price & Wang 2024). Variants in *fepE* may increase virulence if it causes gain of function. Based on its 
ontology information, *mdtK* encodes a xenobiotic transporter that mediates responses to antibiotics and acts as an antiporter that transports sodium ions. *mdtK* variants may cause increased antibiotic 
resistance by allowing recognition and transport of new antibiotics. Overall, the genome SRR39099216 can be characterized by possible increases in virulence and antibiotic resistance, as well as possible 
alterations in respiration and biofilm formation.

Genome SRR38876376 exhibits possible unique changes in pyrimidine metabolism, pilus assembly, transport of xenobiotics, DNA repair, transcription regulation, as well RNA repair and processing, 
based on ontologies of genes variants of which are exclusive to this genome. Ontologies of genes *arcA*, *norR*, and *umuD* indicate that they regulate transcription, with norR being a positive regulator 
of anaerobic nitric oxide reductase and umuD acting as a negative regulator of transcription. This genome also has variants for two transporter-encoding genes, namely *mdtC* and *ynfM*. *ynfM* encodes a 
putative transporter in *S*. Typhimurium, while *mdtC* encodes the MdtC component of the MdtB2C trimeric multidrug efflux pump (Kim & Nikaido 2012). Variants in these two genes likely indicate changes in 
transport ability, such as specificity to certain substrates. Variants in mdtC likely confer resistance to specific antibiotics.

Genome SRR38895580 is marked by variants in genes *iaaA*, *phoE*, *steC*, and *ydiF*. *iaaA* encodes isoaspartyl peptidase, which has proteolytic activity by way of asparaginase and beta-aspartyl peptidase 
mechanisms, based on its gene ontology. *phoE* encodes PhoE, an outer membrane porin, with a gene ontology of monoatomic ion transport. This pore protein is involved in phosphate assimilation, and expression is 
induced by phosphate starvation (Majumdar, Ghatak & Ghosh 2005). *steC* encodes the secreted effector kinase SteC, which causes the formation of actin filaments around vacuoles within the host cells containing *S*. 
Typhimurium cells, possibly aiding intracellular replication of *S*. Typhimurium (Poh et al. 2008). Variants in *phoE* and *steC* may cause alterations in phosphate uptake and intracellular replication, affecting 
survival in low-phosphate environment as well as intracellular virulence. *ydiF*, based on its gene ontology and UniProt entry, encodes acetate-CoA transferase, an enzyme involved in ketone body catabolism. 
A KEGG pathway (ID stm00627) indicates that this enzyme is involved in aminobenzoate degradation. Therefore, this genome likely possesses alterations in catabolism of aminobenzoates due to a variant in *ydiF*.

Variants that characterize the genome ERR023773 are of the genes *patA*, *phnT*, *prfB*, *trmL*, and *yajI*. *patA* encodes putrescine transaminase, which catalyzes the first step in catabolism of putrescine when induced by 
nitrogen limitation (Schneider, Hernandez & Reitzer 2013). Based on their gene ontologies, *phnT* encodes a putative 2-aminoethylphosphonate import ATP-binding protein, *prfB* encodes the peptide chain release factor 2, 
*trmL* encodes tRNA (cytidine(34)-2'-O)-methyltransferase, and *yajI* encodes an outer membrane lipoprotein. The product of *prfB* catalyzes the release of new polypeptide chains from bacterial ribosomes during termination 
of translation via hydrolysis (Michael, Huang, Ray, Kinz-Thompson & Gonzalez 2025). The *trmL* gene has ontologies of wobble position cytidine and uridine ribose methylation, with tRNA as the binding target. Taken together, 
gene variants exclusive to this genome may indicate alterations or alternative mechanisms in metabolism under nitrogen-limited conditions as well as protein synthesis.

Genome SRR10178266 is also uniquely characterized by variants of genes pertaining to information processing, albeit in DNA repair and replication instead. *dinB* encodes DNA polymerase IV, which, based on gene ontology information, 
functions in DNA repair and replication. *ydgG* encodes flap exonuclease Xni, with gene ontologies in DNA replication and Okazaki fragment processing.  *sseL* encodes deubiquitinase SseL with a gene ontology in proteolysis. 
SseL removes ubiquitin from ubiquitylated proteins in infected macrophages and exhibit delayed cytotoxicity to these cells (Rytkönen et al. 2007). Ubiquitin removal from protein aggregates near intracellular *S*. Typhimurium prevents 
recognition and destruction of *S*. Typhimurium cells via autophagy (Mesquita et al. 2012). *sbmA* encodes an inner membrane protein prevalent in enteric bacteria that acts as a peptide transporter, possibly linked to an ecological niche 
in the host’s gut (Giulia et al. 2013). Variants in *sseL* and *sbmA* present in this genome indicate the possibility of altered virulence against macrophages and specificity for certain peptides in transport.

Genomic markers apparent in the genome SRR38943380 consist of variants of *bisC*, *ftsX*, *phsC*, and *ydiN*. The presence of *bisC* and *phsC* variants indicate possible alterations in cellular respiration and cytochrome activity, as these genes 
have ontologies in electron transfer activity, with *bisC* also exhibiting an ontology in cytochrome c activity and phsC encoding thiosulfate reductase cytochrome B subunit gamma. In particular, the biological process gene ontology of *bisC* 
is anaerobic respiration. *ftsX* encodes a cell division protein with a gene ontology also pertaining to cell division, indicating that this genome likely has a distinct cell division mechanism. *ydiN* encodes a transporter that likely transports 
intermediates or products of carbohydrate metabolism pathways, such as the shikimate pathway (Khalfaoui-Hassani, Blaby-Haas, Verissimo & Daldal 2023; Liu, Omar, Abrahante, Nagaraja & Vidovic 2020).
## Conclusion
The presence of unique high-impact gene variants within *S*. Typhimurium genomes from different geographical sampling locations reflects the diversity of molecular mechanisms, ecological niches, and environmental pressures faced by these genomes. 
Environmental differences in geographical locations where each genome is sampled allow the persistence of variants of genes pertaining to specific functions such as involvement in specific metabolic pathways, regulation of gene expression, defense 
against viruses or antibiotics, transport of various substances across membranes, biofilm formation, or various methods of virulence. Therefore, adaptations and alternate mechanisms to these functions that arise due to these gene variants can be 
inferred from gene variants, whether they are shared by different *S*. Typhimurium populations or unique to a specific population.
